**Interbrain synchrony** refers to the phenomenon in which the brains of two or more individuals show correlated patterns of neural activity during social interaction. It reflects **neural coupling** between individuals and underlies social bonding, [empathy](../../social-media/linkedin/spectrum_of_empathy.md), joint attention, and mutual understanding.

## Mechanisms

The listener's brain activity mirrors the speaker's with a short temporal lag, driven by **predictive processing**: the receiving brain continuously models the sender's neural dynamics.

- **Frequency bands**: Coupling spans theta (4–8 Hz, memory encoding), alpha (8–13 Hz, attentional gating), and gamma (30–80 Hz, feature binding)
- **Phase synchrony**: The dominant form — relative phase alignment between oscillators, not merely amplitude correlation
- **Face-to-face modulation**: Eye contact amplifies synchrony; mediated or text-based communication shows reduced coupling in prefrontal and superior temporal regions

## Hyperscanning

**Hyperscanning** simultaneously records from two or more brains to quantify synchrony. Pioneered by Uri Hasson (Princeton, 2004) using dual fMRI: a speaker narrates a story whilst the listener's neural response is cross-correlated with the speaker's activity in real time.

| Method | Temporal res. | Spatial res. | Best use case |
|--------|--------------|-------------|---------------|
| EEG | Milliseconds | Low (~64–256 ch) | High temporal precision, mobile |
| fNIRS | ~2 s | Surface cortex | Naturalistic social settings |
| Dual fMRI | ~2 s | Whole brain | Spatial precision, constrained lab |
| Dual MEG | Milliseconds | Whole brain | Highest fidelity, rare infrastructure |

## Key Findings

- **Speaker–listener coupling predicts comprehension**: Higher synchrony → better comprehension scores (Hasson 2010); coupling collapses in noise or across language barriers
- **Prefrontal and TPJ dominance**: Strongest synchrony in right prefrontal cortex and temporoparietal junction — regions for theory of mind and social prediction
- **Teacher–student learning**: Classroom fNIRS synchrony predicts learning outcomes; students who synchronise more with their teacher show better retention (Bevilacqua et al., 2019)
- **Musical ensemble**: Motor and premotor cortex synchrony peaks during joint performance, even without explicit verbal communication
- **Therapeutic alliance**: Therapist–patient prefrontal synchrony correlates with therapeutic outcome across sessions

## Theoretical Frameworks

| Framework | Core claim | Proponent |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Neural coupling | Communication is brain-to-brain coupling; shared meaning = shared neural state | Hasson |
| Active inference | Two agents sharing a Markov blanket form a higher-order coupled system; synchrony is its signature | Friston |
| Embodied / extended mind | Synchrony is evidence that cognition is not skull-bound | Clark, Chalmers |

The active inference framing connects most directly to [PSNST](../../_general/psnst.md): if two brains form a joint Markov blanket, the coupled system may instantiate a distributed phenomenal structure beyond individual [integrated information](../../videos/integrated_information_theory.md).

## Clinical Relevance

- **Autism spectrum**: Reduced interbrain synchrony during face-processing and language exchange; candidate biomarker of social communication difficulties
- **Depression**: Diminished synchrony in peer and therapeutic contexts; may sustain social withdrawal via reduced interpersonal predictability
- **Motor rehabilitation**: Neural entrainment leveraged in stroke rehab — synchronised movement training exploits motor/premotor coupling

## Consciousness Engineering Implications

Interbrain synchrony challenges individualist accounts of consciousness. If phenomenal states are substrate-bound but interaction-modulated, synchrony may represent transient **cross-subject integration**: two Markov-blanket-coupled systems briefly constituting a higher-order phenomenal whole. For biomimetic neural interfaces, the key question is whether a neuromorphic node can participate in this coupling. Evidence that synchrony depends on bidirectional prediction-error propagation — not merely correlated output — suggests any substrate capable of running active inference could in principle synchronise with biological tissue.
