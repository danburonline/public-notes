#fundamental/communication

Appraising the evidence **means evaluating the quality, relevance, and reliability of information, data, or research findings before using them to make a decision.** It is the disciplined step between reading a paper and trusting its conclusion. Whether the context is clinical practice, grant writing, engineering design, or policy, the goal is the same: ensure that decisions rest on the best available evidence rather than on authority, novelty, or confirmation bias.

## Core Questions

A useful appraisal can be reduced to five questions:

1. **What is the question?** — Is the research question clearly defined, answerable, and relevant to the decision at hand?
2. **What design was used?** — Does the study design match the question? Randomised trials for causal claims, observational studies for associations, mechanistic work for plausibility.
3. **Is the methodology sound?** — Are the sample, controls, measurements, and analyses appropriate and reported transparently?
4. **What do the results actually show?** — Look past the abstract: effect sizes, confidence intervals, absolute risks, and statistical vs. practical significance.
5. **Are the conclusions justified?** — Do the authors' claims follow from the data, or do they overreach?

## Hierarchy of Evidence

Evidence is not all equal. A rough hierarchy, from strongest to weakest for causal questions:

- **Systematic reviews and meta-analyses** of well-conducted randomised controlled trials
- **Randomised controlled trials (RCTs)** with adequate allocation concealment and blinding
- **Cohort and case-control studies** — useful when randomisation is unethical or impossible
- **Case series and expert opinion** — hypothesis-generating, not decisive
- **Mechanistic reasoning** — explains *how* something might work, not *whether* it works in vivo

Design quality matters more than design prestige. A biased RCT can be less informative than a well-conducted cohort study.

## Critical Appraisal Frameworks

Several formal tools help structure appraisal:

- **[GRADE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GRADE_approach)** — rates the certainty of evidence across outcomes, widely used in clinical guidelines.
- **[CASP](https://www.casp-uk.net/) (Critical Appraisal Skills Programme)** — checklists tailored to randomised trials, cohort studies, case-control studies, and qualitative research.
- **[CONSORT](http://www.consort-statement.org/)** and **[PRISMA](http://prisma-statement.org/)** — reporting standards for trials and systematic reviews; a study that ignores them is harder to appraise.

## Common Biases and Pitfalls

- **Publication bias** — positive results are more likely to be published, distorting the literature.
- **P-hacking** — selective reporting, subgroup dredging, or stopping rules that inflate false positives.
- **Surrogate endpoint trap** — a treatment improves a biomarker but not the patient-important outcome.
- **Confounding** — an observed association is caused by a third, unmeasured variable.
- **Confirmation bias** — seeking evidence that supports a preferred conclusion while dismissing contradictory findings.
- **Authority bias** — trusting a claim because of the institution or journal it came from.
