The **access/phenomenal consciousness distinction**, introduced by [Ned Block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ned_Block) (1995), divides conscious mental life into two dissociable components: the information available for reasoning and report (access consciousness), and the qualitative "what it is like" character of experience (phenomenal consciousness). The distinction is central to philosophy of mind, empirical consciousness research, and the engineering programme of [consciousness engineering](../_general/consciousness_engineering.md) because it raises the question: if we preserve function, have we preserved _experience_?

## Access Consciousness

Access consciousness refers to the type of information that is readily available to cognitive processes for reasoning, decision making, and verbal report. It is the aspect of consciousness that allows us to access and utilise information in a way that influences our actions and decisions. Key characteristics include:

- **Awareness and Processing:** Involves awareness of information and its active processing.
- **Cognitive Utilisation:** Enables reasoning, decision-making, and the ability to verbally report information.
- **Functionality:** Central to tasks that require attention, memory recall, and executive functions.

Access consciousness is the functional layer that [consciousness engineering](../_general/consciousness_engineering.md) aims to preserve across substrate transitions — ensuring cognitive continuity does not require the original biological hardware. It is also the dimension of consciousness most readily operationalisable: the [Perturbational Complexity Index](../books/the_feeling_of_life_itself/neural_correlate_of_consciousness.md) (PCI, Casali et al. 2013) measures the brain's capacity for integrated information processing — access consciousness by another name.

Because access consciousness is defined functionally, it raises no special problem for [multiple realisability](../books/how_to_build_a_brain/multiple_realisability.md): any system satisfying the relevant functional profile — biological, artificial, or hybrid — counts, in principle, as access-conscious.

## Phenomenal Consciousness

Phenomenal consciousness, in contrast, is the subjective experience of sensory perceptions, emotions, and feelings. It represents the qualitative, experiential aspect of consciousness, often referred to as the "what it is like" aspect (Nagel, 1974) of being conscious. Key characteristics include:

- **Subjective Experience:** Deals with personal, qualitative experiences of sensations and emotions.
- **Sensory Perceptions:** Includes the raw feel of sensory inputs, such as the redness of red or the pain of a headache.
- **Emotional Feelings:** Encompasses the full range of emotional experiences, from joy to sorrow.

The [neural correlate of consciousness](../books/the_feeling_of_life_itself/neural_correlate_of_consciousness.md) research programme empirically searches for the minimal neural mechanisms sufficient for phenomenal experience. [Integrated information theory](integrated_information_theory.md) attempts to quantify it via Φ (phi): the degree to which a system generates integrated information irreducible to its parts. PCI uses TMS-evoked cortical responses to estimate Φ — an _access-based_ measurement of a _phenomenal_ target.

> [!Examples]
> **Access Consciousness Examples:**
>
> 1. **Reading**: The ability to read and comprehend a book.
> 2. **Recollection**: Remembering past events or information.
> 3. **Problem Solving**: Using logic and reasoning to solve a complex problem.
>
> **Phenomenal Consciousness Examples:**
>
> 1. **Perception of Colour**: Experiencing the vibrant colours of a sunset.
> 2. **Taste Sensation**: Enjoying the flavour of a delicious meal.
> 3. **Emotional Response**: Feeling joy, sadness, or fear in response to a situation.

## The Explanatory Gap

The distinction matters because the two forms appear to come apart. [Philosophical zombies](../_general/philosophical_zombies.md) (Chalmers, 1996) are the canonical argument: hypothetical beings physically and functionally identical to conscious humans — fully access-conscious — yet with no phenomenal experience whatsoever. If p-zombies are conceivable, then functional specification does not logically entail phenomenal consciousness.

This is the **explanatory gap** (Levine, 1983): why physical information-processing gives rise to subjective experience at all. Access consciousness is a functional kind and thus tractable to functional explanation. Phenomenal consciousness resists the same treatment, producing the sense that something extra is required — a perspective that leads naturally into:

- **[Strong emergence](strong_emergence.md).** If phenomenal consciousness is strongly emergent from (rather than merely reducible to) physical processes, then multiple realisability of the phenomenal mind is not guaranteed — the specific physical substrate may matter constitutively.
- **[Supervenience](../books/taxonomy_and_metaphysics_of_mind-uploading/supervenience.md).** Access consciousness may supervene on function; phenomenal consciousness may require a stronger, perhaps locally physical, dependence relation.
- **[Biological naturalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Searle).** Searle's position: phenomenal consciousness is causally produced by specific biological processes — functionally identical silicon would lack it, even if access-matched.
- **[Fading qualia](../books/from_biological_to_artificial_consciousness/fading_qualia.md) (Chalmers, 1995).** The gradual replacement thought experiment argues that if access is preserved throughout a substrate transition, phenomenal experience must also be preserved — otherwise the subject's introspective reports would dissociate from their experience, an implausible split.

## Why the Distinction Matters for Substrate Transfer

For [consciousness engineering](../_general/consciousness_engineering.md) and its flagship protocol, [Progressive Synthetic Neural Substrate Transfer](../_general/psnst.md), the access/phenomenal distinction is the criterion of success:

| What is transferred | Verdict | Evidence required |
|---|---|---|
| Access consciousness only | Zombie emulator — functional but not the person | Behavioural equivalence, cognitive task matching |
| Access + phenomenal consciousness | Genuine continuity of self | PCI preservation, behavioural equivalence, introspective report consistency |

If phenomenal consciousness is strongly emergent from biological tissue, then any purely functional substrate transfer (however high-fidelity) preserves access while losing experience — the transferred entity becomes a p-zombie. If Chalmers' organisational invariance holds, then preserving functional organisation preserves everything that matters.

The fading qualia argument supports the latter, but the argument is philosophical, not empirical — the gap remains contested.

## How Theories Handle the Gap

Different theories of consciousness treat the access/phenomenal distinction differently, and this affects whether they license substrate independence:

- **[Integrated information theory](integrated_information_theory.md) (Tononi).** The cause-effect structure specified by the system's intrinsic causal powers _is_ the experience. Φ quantifies phenomenal level, but access (global availability) is a separate property. Both depend on the intrinsic causal structure of the physical substrate — so merely input-output equivalent systems may differ in Φ.
- **Global workspace theory (Baars, Dehaene).** Consciousness IS global broadcast — the difference between access and phenomenal collapses. If you've replicated the global workspace dynamics, you've replicated consciousness.
- **Higher-order theories (Rosenthal, Lau).** Phenomenal = access to a higher-order representation of one's own mental state. The gap is bridged by metacognition.
- **Biological naturalism (Searle).** The access/phenomenal distinction is an artefact: all consciousness is phenomenal, and it is a biological phenomenon like digestion. There is no gap to close — biology just does it.
