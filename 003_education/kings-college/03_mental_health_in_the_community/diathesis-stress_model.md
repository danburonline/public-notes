The **Diathesis-[Stress](stress.md) Model** proposes that mental disorders arise from the interaction between a person's [predisposition](../../../001_private/_general/predisposition.md) or vulnerability (diathesis) and stressful life events. Individuals with higher diathesis require less stress to trigger a disorder, while those with lower diathesis require more. The model is commonly applied to explain depression, anxiety, and schizophrenia.

## Components

- **Diathesis**: Genetic, biological, or psychological vulnerability. Can be constitutional (e.g., inherited traits) or acquired (e.g., early trauma). Examples: family history of mental illness, neurotransmitter imbalances, maladaptive cognitive schemas.
- **Stress**: Environmental triggers that activate the underlying vulnerability. Can be acute (e.g., job loss, bereavement) or chronic (e.g., ongoing poverty, caregiving burden).

> [!tip] Clinical Implications
> The model suggests two intervention pathways:
> 
> 1. **Reduce diathesis**: Pharmacotherapy, early intervention programmes, building resilience
> 2. **Reduce stress**: Social support, stress management, removing environmental triggers

> [!warning] Limitations
> - Oversimplifies gene-environment interaction
> - Difficult to quantify diathesis levels empirically
> - Does not account for protective factors (e.g., social support buffering stress)
