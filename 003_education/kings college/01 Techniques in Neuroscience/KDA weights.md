In cell biology, “KDA” refers to “kiloDalton” or “kilodalton,” which is a **unit of measurement for the molecular weight of proteins and other macromolecules.** One kilodalton equals 1,000 Daltons, which is a unit of atomic mass equal to one-twelfth of the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

Here are some drop‑in Obsidian callouts you can paste into the note.

> [!summary] Quick reference
> - kDa (kilodalton) is a unit of molecular mass commonly used for proteins.
> - $1\ \text{kDa} = 10^3\ \text{Da}$ and numerically $1\ \text{Da} \approx 1\ \text{g mol}^{-1}$.
> - Example conversions:
>   - $50\ \text{kDa} = 50{,}000\ \text{Da} \approx 50{,}000\ \text{g mol}^{-1}$.
>   - $150\ \text{kDa}$ (IgG) $\approx 150{,}000\ \text{g mol}^{-1}$.

> [!example] Typical protein sizes (handy ladder anchors)
> - β‑actin ≈ 42 kDa
> - GAPDH ≈ 36–37 kDa
> - Tubulin (α/β) ≈ 50–55 kDa
> - GFP ≈ 27 kDa
> - Albumin ≈ 66 kDa
> - IgG (heavy chain ≈ 50 kDa, light chain ≈ 25 kDa; intact ≈ 150 kDa)
> - RNA Pol II (largest subunit) ≈ 220 kDa
